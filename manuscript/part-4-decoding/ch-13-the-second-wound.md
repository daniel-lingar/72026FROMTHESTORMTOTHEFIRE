# Chapter 13: The Second Wound

*They always made it sound simple. “It’s all in your head. You’re making yourself worse. Be a man. Grow up.”*

*They said it like shame was medicine. Like humiliation was a ladder. But that is not how a nervous system heals. Those words didn’t make me stronger; they made me disappear.*

*There is the first wound: the incubator, the violence, the losses, the drugs. Then there is the second wound: what people say about you after it happened.*

*The first wound is the injury. The second wound is the stigma. And stigma doesn’t sit outside the body. It crawls under the skin. It changes the way you breathe. It makes every mistake feel like proof that you are broken.*

### “It’s All in Your Head”

People say “it’s all in your head” like that proves it is not real. But where do they think terror lives? Where do they think memory lives?

Of course it is in my head. But it is also in my stomach when it drops for no reason. It is in my jaw when it locks, my chest when it tightens, and my hands when they shake. It is in the way my body knows danger before my mind has words for it.

Complex PTSD is not imagination. It is injury. It is the nervous system treating today like yesterday because yesterday never got resolved. When someone says, “It’s all in your head,” what they are really saying is, “I do not want to understand this.” And when you hear that enough, you stop trying to explain. You begin to wonder if maybe you are crazy. That is how stigma works. It does not have to prove anything. It only has to make you doubt yourself.

### “You’re Making Yourself Worse”

This one always sounded like responsibility. Like I was standing there with a shovel, digging my own grave on purpose. Like I enjoyed the panic, or the depression, or destroying good things.

People love to point at the pattern after it has already become a problem, but they do not ask what it protected. Avoidance made sense when facing things meant being overwhelmed. Isolation made sense when people were unsafe. Numbing made sense when feeling everything would have killed me.

Those patterns were not random. They were not proof that I chose chaos over family. They were survival strategies that stayed past their expiration date. A thing can save you once and destroy you later. That is why healing is so hard: you are not just giving up bad habits; you are letting go of the only things that once kept you alive.

Shame does not teach a nervous system safety. Shame teaches hiding. Shame teaches lying. Shame teaches relapse. A better sentence would have been: “I can see this is hurting you now, even if it helped you survive before.” That sentence leaves a door open. The other one shuts it.

### “Be a Man”

Nothing has killed more men quietly than that phrase.

Be a man.

Three words. A whole prison.

It means do not cry. Do not shake. Do not admit fear. Do not say you are lonely, or drowning, or ashamed. Just work. Provide. Shut up. Take it. Drink it down. Smoke it down. And if silence starts killing you, at least die without making anyone uncomfortable.

That is what “be a man” really teaches. It doesn’t create strength; it creates emotional starvation. It teaches boys to become men who cannot name their pain until it comes out sideways as rage, addiction, or a body always braced for war. C-PTSD does not disappear because a man refuses to cry. It goes underground. It grows teeth. Then it comes out in the parts of life he wanted most to protect: his marriage, his children, his work, his body.

Real manhood is not silence. Real manhood is facing the truth without running from it. A man asking for help is not failing at being a man. He is refusing to let shame write the ending.

### “Grow Up” & “Aren’t You Tired of Being a Nobody?”

People say “grow up” when they think the problem is maturity. But trauma does not care how old you are. You can have bills, jobs, kids, and gray in your beard, and still have parts of you frozen in rooms nobody ever rescued you from. When someone says “grow up,” they are yelling at a survival state. They are shaming the part of a person that actually needs the most care.

And “Aren’t you tired of being a nobody?” goes straight for the soul. A person with C-PTSD does not need help feeling like a failure. We already carry invisible scoreboards of every lost job, relapse, and broken relationship. Calling someone a nobody doesn’t motivate them. It confirms the lie trauma already planted: that their life has no weight. No one heals by being convinced they are worthless. People heal when they can believe there is still something in them worth saving.

### Shame: The Wound Under the Wound

Guilt says, “I did something wrong.” Shame says, “I am wrong.”

Guilt can lead to repair. Shame leads to hiding. C-PTSD is full of deep, identity-level shame—the kind that says your very existence is a problem. When direct stigma gives that voice a microphone, outside voices become inside voices.

Sometimes suicidal thinking does not arrive wearing the face of death. Sometimes it arrives wearing the face of logic. It tells you it would be merciful to leave. It tells you your absence would be a gift. That is not truth. That is shame talking in a calm voice.

Nobody wants to talk about this part directly, but we have to. Direct stigma can push a person closer to suicide. Not always by itself, but when those sentences stack on top of years of trauma, grief, isolation, and exhaustion, they become part of the weight that breaks a person.

Sometimes suicide isn't a loud crisis. Sometimes it’s a man sitting alone, staring at nothing, feeling like he has already become a ghost. Sometimes it’s him laughing with people during the day and fighting not to die at night. If you are reading this and you know that place, hear me clearly: You do not have to fix your whole life tonight. You only have to stay. One breath. One phone call. One moment of not letting shame make the final decision. Healing is slower and messier, but it leaves you here to see what can still be rebuilt.

### The Lie of Tough Love

A lot of people hide cruelty behind “tough love.” But honesty without compassion is often just violence with better manners.

Real tough love has boundaries; fake tough love has contempt. Real tough love says, “I love you, but I will not enable what is destroying you.” Fake tough love says, “You are pathetic.”

People with C-PTSD do not need lies, and they do not need coddling. They need truth. But truth does not have to strip a person of dignity. Accountability says, “You did harm. Now take the next right step.” Stigma says, “You are harm.” One gives a person a road. The other gives them a grave.

They could have said, “I don’t fully understand what is happening inside you, but I believe it is real.” They could have said, “You do not have to hide pain to prove you are a man.”

Sometimes one word matters. *Stay.* Not “get over it.” Not “man up.” Just: *Stay.*

### The Truth

The truth is, C-PTSD is not weakness. It is what happens when survival gets wired so deeply that peace feels foreign. Healing does not come from more shame. It comes from truth with dignity, and accountability with compassion.

A person with C-PTSD needs the world to understand that harm and wound can live in the same body. They need someone to say, “You are responsible for what you do next,” and also, “You are not beyond saving.”

And for a man who has spent his whole life believing shame was the final truth, that sentence can become the first crack of light.

***

**CLINICAL TAKEAWAYS & SYSTEMIC SHIFTS**

**For the Clinician:**
*   **Stigma is a physiological stressor:** When a client with C-PTSD hears “it's all in your head” or “be a man,” it is not just an emotional insult. It is a nervous system threat that triggers the exact same freeze/shame cascade as the original trauma.
*   **Differentiate shame from guilt:** Guilt says "I did something wrong" (leads to repair). Shame says "I am wrong" (leads to hiding, relapse, and suicide). Treatment must target identity-level shame, not just behavioral guilt.
*   **Male presentation of trauma:** Men with C-PTSD often do not present with "classic" emotional flooding. They present with irritability, workaholism, substance use, emotional starvation, and physical shutdown. "Being a man" is often a trauma response disguised as masculinity.

**For the System (Legal/Probation/Casework):**
*   **Compliance vs. Capacity:** A missed appointment or a frozen response in court is rarely deliberate defiance. It is often executive dysfunction and a trauma-induced freeze response. Punishing the freeze with more pressure only guarantees a deeper shutdown.
*   **The danger of "Tough Love":** Confrontational, shaming approaches ("You're making yourself worse," "Grow up") do not build accountability in a traumatized nervous system. They build secrecy. Accountability requires dignity; shame builds defense and relapse.

*I didn’t need to be fixed. I needed to be understood. Then I could fix myself.*
