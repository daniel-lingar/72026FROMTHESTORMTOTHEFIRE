Chapter 8: Anatomy of a Spiral
Trinity, Tina, Kristina — Dissecting the Damage
This wasn’t three love stories.
It was one wound wearing three different faces.
For years, I kept those names in separate boxes so I wouldn’t have to look at the pattern straight on. Trinity was the marriage. Tina was the escape. Kristina was the mind game. Three different women. Three different disasters. Three different explanations for why everything in my life kept catching fire.
That version was easier to live with because it let me keep pretending each wreck was self-contained. If every collapse belonged to a different room, then maybe there was no architecture connecting them. Maybe I was just unlucky. Maybe the whole thing was random.
It wasn’t random.
The women were different. The dynamics were different. The damage took different shapes. But the wound underneath was the same. Every one of those relationships plugged into the same circuitry: abandonment, shame, panic, self-erasure, and the deep animal terror that connection could disappear at any second.
I did not move from one relationship to the next because I was learning. I moved because I was repeating. I was carrying the same raw wiring into different rooms and calling each fire by a different name.
This chapter is not a blame list. It is not a courtroom. It is not a plea for sympathy.
It is an autopsy.
Trinity: The Training Ground
Trinity was not the beginning of my damage, but she became the longest environment my damage ever lived in.
When I met her, I was still young enough to confuse responsibility with readiness. A pregnancy came fast, and the part of me that had grown up around chaos latched onto the idea that I could outrun my own instability by building something solid on the outside. Work hard. Pay bills. Get the trailer. Get the car. Show up. Be a dad. Be useful. If the frame looked stable enough, maybe nobody would notice the fire inside it.
From the outside, it probably looked like a life. Inside it, the static never stopped.
The deeper damage didn’t come from the fighting. It came from what happened to my sense of reality inside it. Any time I tried to bring pain into the room, the room turned on me. Grief became selfishness. Confusion became weakness. Panic became immaturity. If I said I was drowning, I got handed a speech about how someone else had it worse.
That kind of invalidation is not just hurtful. It is instructional. It teaches you which parts of yourself are unacceptable. It teaches you to scan your own feelings before you speak and ask not, “Is this true?” but, “Will this get used against me?” Over time, that question becomes your real personality.
I began editing myself before I even spoke. I started shrinking in real time. I learned how to swallow reactions, delay needs, soften truths, and disappear inside conversations that were supposed to be intimate. The safest version of me became the least visible one.
And the kids made that system almost impossible to leave. People look at the marriage from the outside and ask why I stayed. They assume I stayed because I believed in the relationship. The truth is uglier and more nervous-system-driven than that. I stayed because my connection to my children had become braided into the relationship’s control structure. The thought of losing them did not feel like heartbreak. It felt like annihilation. Every threat, every separation, every hint that access to my kids could be restricted hit a place in me that was older than reason. It was body terror.
The most humiliating symbol of that whole period was also the most honest.
I hid in closets. Not metaphorically. Literally.
There were times I stayed out of sight in my own life because being acknowledged felt more dangerous than being erased. Her family might come around and I would make myself invisible. She could keep me blocked publicly, keep the image she wanted intact, while I physically shrank into whatever hidden space was available. We were married. We had children together. And I was hiding like my existence needed to be managed.
That is not just dysfunction. That is embodiment. A child who once learned that survival meant being still had grown into a man who could disappear on command if he thought it would preserve attachment. The closet was not a symbol I invented later. It was a room my nervous system recognized immediately.
The marriage did not create my trauma. But it trained it. Refined it. Confirmed it. By the end, the worst thing the marriage had taken from me was not peace. It was trust in my own perceptions. And when a man stops trusting his own perceptions, he becomes easy to steer.
Tina: The Starvation Hook
By the time Tina entered the picture, I was not making decisions from stability. I was making them from collapse.
People talk about love like it happens on neutral ground. But a lot of what gets called love does not begin in clarity. Sometimes it begins in emergency. Sometimes it begins when somebody has been starving so long that relief itself starts to feel sacred.
That was the condition I was in. I was carrying years of shame, an addiction that had become a regulator, and the growing terror of losing my family structure. I was not looking at the world with a steady nervous system and asking who was healthy for me. I was gasping for something that did not feel like drowning.
When a person enters your life at that exact point, your body does not calmly assess them. It lunges.
That is what Tina became to me. Not because she was magic. But because she arrived in the middle of collapse, and collapse changes the meaning of attention. A person who makes room for you when you feel erased can look like salvation. A person who listens when you feel disbelieved can look like destiny. When you are emotionally starving, relief can feel like love.
I didn’t bond from steadiness. I bonded from panic. The part of me attaching was not the calm adult evaluating what a relationship could actually hold. It was the abandoned part. The flooded part. The part that believed one more loss might split me open for good.
That kind of attachment always carries distortion with it. Attention gets overread. Availability gets romanticized. Relief gets mistaken for compatibility. The body says, “I can breathe here,” and the mind rushes to build a future around that first exhale.
I made Tina larger than reality because I needed her to be larger than reality. I needed the connection to mean something big enough to justify the risk, the disloyalty, the rupture. I needed the story to be rescue, because if it wasn’t rescue, then maybe it was just another symptom of how desperate I had become.
Desperation can make a person feel profound. Not because the feelings are fake, but because urgency amplifies everything. A starving nervous system is not a reliable narrator. It can take a little bit of warmth and turn it into revelation.
I don’t say that to cheapen what Tina was. I say it to tell the truth about what I was. I was not choosing from freedom. I was choosing from fear. And fear, when it is dressed up like hope, can be one of the most convincing forces in the world.
Kristina: The Hall of Mirrors
Kristina hit different because she landed where my mind was weakest.
If Trinity had trained me to doubt myself, and Tina had met me in raw need, Kristina stepped into the territory where being seen and being studied started to blur.
What pulled me in first was recognition. She seemed to understand things that mattered to me. Reflected interests back at me. Mirrored emotional language in ways that made the connection feel unusually specific. For a person who had spent years feeling erased, that kind of resonance can feel supernatural.
But being mirrored is not always the same thing as being known. Sometimes it is access.
The parts of me most hungry to be recognized were the same parts most vulnerable to manipulation. If somebody touched that wound the right way, they did not just get my attention. They got my hunger. And hunger is easy to guide.
Gaslighting works best when the ground is already unstable. By the time that relationship formed, I had years of self-doubt behind me. Years of being told I was overreacting. Years of second-guessing what I felt. So when contradictions showed up, when signals got crossed, I did not anchor myself in my own experience. I folded inward and went hunting for what was wrong with me.
That is how a person can end up trapped in confusion that looks invisible from the outside. You replay conversations. Reconstruct tone. Analyze pauses. You stop asking, “Is this treatment acceptable?” and start asking, “Why am I so affected by this?” That is a devastating shift because it relocates the whole problem into your own mind while the dynamic keeps cutting you.
The relationship became a hall of mirrors. I could not tell whether I was seeing clearly or projecting. I kept returning to the very dynamic that was intensifying the confusion in hopes that it would also provide clarity. That is not connection. That is captivity with emotional language wrapped around it.
But I was not passive in it. I brought my own fire into that room. I brought obsession, projection, savior-hunger, abandonment terror, and the impossible demand that another person help me organize pain that had started decades before they arrived. No relationship can safely carry that much historical weight. When it tries, the structure warps.
Kristina did not create the belief that I was fundamentally broken. She touched it. That was enough. Once that belief gets activated, everything grows larger than the facts. The present gets drowned by all the rooms it accidentally opened.
The Spiral
For a long time, I used these women like evidence in a trial. See? That’s why I used. That’s why I ran. That’s why I broke.
But if I stop the story there, then all I have done is rename my suffering and hand the power for it entirely to other people. The more useful truth is harder and less flattering.
The common denominator was me.
Not because I was the villain in every room. But because I carried the same unhealed wiring into each relationship and then acted shocked when it produced familiar fires.
That wiring had rules:
When I felt unseen, I latched onto whoever made me feel recognized.
When I felt threatened with abandonment, I reached like separation might kill me.
When I got confused, I assumed I was defective.
When shame flared, I hid.
When panic rose past what my body could regulate, I used.
When using created more shame, I hid harder.
That is the spiral. Trinity, Tina, and Kristina were not separate detours from the same life. They were three expressions of the same unresolved system trying, failing, and trying again to solve pain through attachment.
What made the spiral so destructive was not just who entered these rooms, but what was already waiting inside them. Old trauma, shame, invalidation, and unregulated fear turned each connection into a test it was never built to pass.
For most of my life, I only knew how to think in extremes: it is all their fault or it is all mine. Neither one gets close enough to the truth to be useful.
The truth is that I was wounded and responsible. Manipulated in some places and destructive in others. Capable of being harmed and fully capable of causing harm. Reaching for love with tools built for survival.
I was not entering relationships with actual relational tools. I was entering them with freeze, fawn, shame, hypervigilance, emotional hunger, and chemical coping. Then I expected intimacy to grow out of that soil. No wonder the roots twisted.
What It Was Really Asking
Under all three of those relationships, one question kept repeating itself in me, even when I did not have language for it:
Can I be close to someone without disappearing?
That was the real question under the marriage. Under the affair. Under the obsession. Under the panic. Under the relapses. Under the hiding.
And because I did not know that was the question, I kept answering it with the wrong equipment. I confused intensity with depth. Relief with safety. Chemistry with rescue. Being studied with being understood. Emotional adrenaline with love.
So I kept stepping into versions of the same fire and telling myself each one was new. It wasn’t new. It was familiar. And familiarity has a dangerous way of impersonating home when trauma has been your earliest architecture.
Turning Toward the Machinery
For a long time, I thought that if I understood this pattern too clearly, all it would do is confirm the ugliest things I already believed about myself. Weak. Broken. Needy. Crazy.
But that is not what happened.
Worse, because I could no longer hide behind randomness. I had to see the repetition. I had to admit I was carrying tinder into every room.
Better, because the pattern gave me something I had almost never had before: a map.
A map does not erase damage. It does not undo betrayals. It does not return lost years, lost trust, or lost versions of yourself. But it does let you stop wandering blind inside the same wreckage.
Once I could see that the spiral was not random, I could stop spending all my energy asking who struck the match and start asking what in me kept making the rooms so flammable.
That question changed everything. Not all at once. Not cleanly. Not enough to save what had already burned. But enough to turn me toward the machinery underneath the story.
Seeing the spiral was only the first step. Naming the pattern didn’t fix it, but it stopped me from pretending every collapse was random. The next step wasn’t finding better people or begging the past to change. It was turning toward the machinery underneath it all—the blueprint, the wiring, the way my nervous system had been set to danger long before any of these relationships showed up.
That’s where the fire really started.
