**Chapter 20: Finding the Map in the Madness**

A while earlier, before everything with Sarah that night, I'd made a stupid decision that nearly cost me everything. Someone had a box of junk at their place—not household trash, just random shit that looked useful. I was the type who'd haul stuff to the dump and come back with more than I left with. I said yeah, I'd take it. On the way I went through it and kept a few things instead of tossing everything.

Weeks later a buddy named Mike saw one of the bags and got quiet. "You better be careful with that," he said. "Those look like blood stains from a murder." I laughed it off at the time. Then months after that, another guy mentioned the FBI had been on some land looking for a body. That's when the panic hit. I remembered Mike's warning and burned the bag in a fire out back. Three days later I heard the knock.

I bolted out the side door and ended up at a house I knew. They let me use the bathroom. Five minutes later someone yelled that I was in there. Two cops: an investigator right in front of me and a deputy from another county positioned on my left flank. The setup felt off, deliberate. The investigator started drilling me. I answered everything straight. He accused me of lying, said he caught me in one, then claimed he'd review the recording. I told him to do it—I wasn't lying. He came back and shifted: maybe I wasn't lying, but I was holding back, knew more than I was saying.

He kept pushing about "the black bag." Over and over. Whether on purpose or not, it triggered me. "The bag was fucking red," I snapped, "and I burned it." I told him someone had given me a box, gave him the broad strokes without names. He drove me to Sarah's place because I mentioned a couple things from the box that hadn't been thrown away—they were in her truck. She wasn't home. She'd cleaned the truck out a few days earlier at the corner store, so we went to the dumpster. It was full. They started moving shit around but when it came time to climb in deeper, the investigator and deputy looked at each other and said they weren't getting in there. We left.

The deputy from the other county drove me home. He got out with a consent form and asked me to initial it so he could search my room. I told him it wasn't my house, he'd need to talk to my parents. He said no, just my room. He started reading the fine print—if he saw anything illegal unrelated to what they were looking for, he could arrest me for it. I hesitated. He reassured me he wasn't there for drugs; if he saw any he'd put them back and act like he didn't see them. I didn't believe him for a second, but refusing meant more cops would show up. I initialed.

He tore my room apart looking for the bag I already told him I burned. Found needles, some bags, a couple balls in a drawer. He shut it like he promised and kept looking. Eventually he left. I had no idea if he was coming back, if I was about to get charged with burning evidence or whatever the fuck they could pin on me.

That started the longest six months of my life. I texted friends, freaking out, but nobody would talk to me. People heard there was a drug bust at my place on the main road and the silence was total. I never felt more alone. I did all the drugs, drank, partied—anything to drown the waiting. Every knock, every car slowing down, I thought this was it. Prison for something that started with a box of junk I should've never kept. Later I heard the guy had a heart attack in the woods by his house and they found the body. I never bought that story, but nothing else ever came of it.

That experience showed me how fast a dumb mistake can snowball when you're already living on the edge. The investigator's mind games—the deliberate positioning, the lie about catching me, the bag color trap—were textbook ways to rattle someone. The consent form was a masterclass in pressure: sign or we bring more heat. The system doesn't need to charge you to punish you; the waiting itself is the sentence. It shattered whatever was left of my nervous system and left me raw as hell by the time Sarah pulled up that night.
