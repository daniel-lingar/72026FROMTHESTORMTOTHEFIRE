From the Storm to the Fire

Working Manuscript by Daniel Lingar

The NICU didn’t just keep me alive. It taught my nervous system that connection means wires, alarms, and glass. They said I was born early, but I’ve always felt late. Seven months in, and the world pulled me out before I was ready.

Three pounds, four ounces of unfinished life dropped into an incubator. No arms around me, no skin against skin. Just machines and a kind of silence that never really leaves you. My mom was there as much as they would let her be.

She wasn’t absent the way people sometimes assume in stories like mine. She came. She stayed. She loved me.

She just wasn’t allowed to hold me the way a mother is supposed to hold her baby. That matters. But so does this: my body still learned separation before it learned comfort. A few days after I was born, my system started shutting down.

They called a priest to my incubator. He gave me last rites while the monitors screamed around him. Then something shifted. My vitals came back.

I stayed. I didn’t understand any of that, obviously. But my nervous system did. It learned that survival was unstable.

Distance came first. Connection came second, if it came at all. I even had a picture once—my brother reaching through the hand holes of the incubator, playing with me through plastic. I don’t know where it went.

I wish I still had it. That image is what I think about when I try to understand how I started: somebody reaching for me through glass. That’s what the beginning felt like, even before I had language for it. The storm outside didn’t take long to find us.

After my parents split, everything changed. New houses. New routines. New versions of normal that never lasted long enough to become real.

One of the men who entered our lives brought violence with him. Not loud all the time. Not obvious from the outside. But when it came, it came hard.

I was six. I learned the sounds first. A voice changing shape. A door hit too hard.

A body moving across a room with the wrong kind of purpose. Then came the crashes. The yelling. My mother’s voice breaking in ways a kid should never hear.

I would pull a blanket over myself and hold still like stillness could make me disappear. The blanket smelled like mildew and old detergent. I twisted it in my fists until my hands hurt. My heart pounded so hard it felt like it might give me away.

Sometimes I stayed in bed. Sometimes I hid in a cabinet. Sometimes I crawled into a closet. Whatever space looked smallest.

Safest. Whatever space felt like it could erase me enough to get me through the night. That was the rule my body learned early: if danger is bigger than you, become smaller. I heard my mom scream.

I heard things break. I heard the sounds of a grown man tearing through a room like he owned the fear in it. Some nights I heard her begging him to stop. Some nights I heard nothing at all after the noise, and that silence scared me worse.

Once, I peeked through a crack and saw her arm hanging wrong. Just wrong. Limp in a way I didn’t have words for then. I knew it meant pain.

I knew it meant I couldn’t fix it. CPS came to the school once and asked questions about bruises. Me and my sister answered like kids who didn’t know there was another way to live. “Only when we are bad.” That was the answer. I thought that was how homes worked.

Some nights we ran. Mom would burst in fast, scared and serious, telling us to get up right then. We would go barefoot into the dark, gravel biting into our feet while the night air cut our lungs open. We would run to my uncle’s house, a big two-story place that felt like a castle.

But even while we were running, I would look back for her. And sometimes she wasn’t right behind us. That panic settled deep. Not just that danger could happen.

That escape could fail. That you could be told to run and still not get away clean. The violence didn’t last forever. The man died when I was still young.

I remember hearing about it and waiting for relief to show up. It didn’t. That taught me something too. Danger doesn’t always leave when the person does.

Sometimes it stays behind in the body. The nights followed me even after the house got quiet. I wet the bed until I was eleven. That kind of shame gets into a kid early.

You start feeling guilty for things your body is doing without your permission. I didn’t understand trauma. I only understood embarrassment. Around ten, sleep itself became another battleground.

I started waking up frozen. Eyes open. Body gone. I could see the room but couldn’t move.

Couldn’t call out. Couldn’t do anything except lie there and feel something heavy pressing the air flat. Sometimes it felt like a presence. Sometimes it just felt like terror with weight on it.

The first time it happened, I thought I was dying. Then it kept happening. It’s called sleep paralysis. Your brain wakes up before your body does.

During REM sleep, your muscles shut down so you don’t act out your dreams. Sometimes that shutdown lingers even after your mind clicks on. For a normal kid, it’s terrifying. For a kid with a nervous system already wired for hypervigilance, it’s a nightmare layered on top of a fire alarm.

The threat detector stays set to sensitive. When normal REM paralysis collides with a trauma-conditioned brain that refuses to go off duty, the result is conscious awareness inside a paralyzed body. It’s the nervous system doing what it was trained to do: stay alive at all costs, even in sleep. Later, the paralysis turned into something else.

People told me I screamed in my sleep. Bloody murder, they said. The kind of scream that made people think somebody was being killed. I usually remembered nothing.

I would wake to shaken shoulders and faces already scared before I had even opened my eyes. It was trauma meeting physiology. Each time my airway collapsed, each time my brain starved for air, my nervous system went straight from freeze into flight, exploding out as screams I never chose. The child who had learned silence as survival was now crying out in the dark.

The boy given last rites in an incubator was decades later still fighting for breath. Those screams were distress calls from every version of me. My conscious mind slept, but my body remembered everything. To them, it sounded like horror.

To me, it felt like waking up late to my own body. I didn’t have words for any of it. All I knew was that night wasn’t safe anymore. I could be lying still, doing nothing, and my body would still find a way to panic.

Silence didn’t protect me. Sleep didn’t protect me. My own bed didn’t protect me. Even in the dark, something in me stayed braced.

That was the shape of my childhood. Not one single event. Not one easy villain. Not one clean wound.

It was glass first. Then fear. Then the lesson that survival meant getting small, staying quiet, and waiting for the danger to pass. The problem is, for some bodies, it never really passes.

Mine just learned how to carry it.

You don’t choose addiction. You choose the nearest thing that turns down the volume. The storm outside died down by my teens, but a raw heat flickered alive inside. Jumpy.

Sharp. Mine. School became a pressure cooker. Hallways thick with shouts, lockers clanging, a hum that crushed my head.

My hands trembled, not from drink but from people—too loud, too near. Social anxiety, I’d name it later. Back then, it was just a buzzing under the surface ready to explode. Palms wet, shirt clinging, I’d dodge their eyes, convinced every glance held judgment.

Booze crept in early. Sips from my uncle’s cans at fourteen, a kid playing big. At sixteen, it roared. A cousin’s trailer, football blasting, table sticky with spills.

He tossed me a Bud Light and said, “Go for it.” I didn’t stop. Beer, Zimas, a tequila shot that scorched. Two hours, all gone. “I drank it,” I mumbled, the room tilting. He jolted up. “Bullshit.” Nope.

I woke on the bathroom floor—tile icy, head by the toilet, puke sour in my throat. “God, kill this, I’ll quit,” I croaked. Lie. It lit the fire. Booze dulled the shakes, made me loose.

I chased it hard, desperate for the quiet it offered inside my head, the brief escape from the constant tension. By seventeen, it ruled. Wild Turkey and vodka, one in each hand, shots swapping, throat raw. Then blood came up.

Red in the sink, ulcers clawing my gut. My own body felt like it was attacking me from the inside. I switched to beer—lighter, less pain. Headaches pulsed, a stab behind my eyes; the world swayed if I moved quick.

I’d slump, can sweating in my grip, knowing it was wrecking me. But I couldn’t stop. The need to numb was stronger than the pain. There was a brief illusion of peace.

When I was thirteen, my parents remarried. Mom and Dad patched things up, and for a minute, it felt like we’d rebuilt the wall after years of shelling. For once, home didn’t feel like a battlefield. But by fifteen, I was burning inside again.

Quiet fire, twitchy and hot. School became a cage—crowded halls, loud voices, too many eyes. I shook like I was trying to hold back an earthquake. So I walked.

Grabbed a GED and told everyone it was for money, for work. Half-truth. The whole truth? I just couldn’t breathe in there anymore.

I moved in with my uncle—a big man, Vietnam vet, Purple Heart on the wall, cold beer always in reach. The music was good. The stories were better. I drank for free, lost in a haze that felt like home.

Then everything broke. His son—my cousin, my blood—took a bullet to the head. Cops called it suicide. I didn’t buy it.

No way. He smiled too much, loved too hard. But this was Johnson County, Arkansas. Three deaths in three months, all linked to the same girl, and nobody batted an eye.

Just another chapter in a town that eats its own. After he died, I unraveled. Suicidal thoughts hit like a freight train in ’99. I spent a month in the hospital.

Quit weed. Got sober. Swore I’d become a cop—get justice, dig out the truth. I interviewed at the jail, spent a few hours shadowing the day shift.

At the end, the guy looked me in the eye and said, “You just want to learn the system so you can help your people when they get locked up.” Wrote me off like I was already dirty. That’s when I learned what small-town judgment really costs. Fifteen years later, I did the digging myself. We were right.

It was murder. But it wasn’t Teddy—it was Johnny. Pulled the trigger while Kris, my cousin’s girlfriend, stood by. She went to the store before it happened, came back all tears and gasps, like she didn’t know.

It was staged. All of it. My uncle shattered. Tried to take his life twice while I was just down the hall.

First time it was pills scattered like confetti across the kitchen table. Second time, he put a .22 in his gut. I called my mom, barely able to speak. “I’m not right,” I choked out. First real panic attack—sweat, gasping, no air.

Childhood fear, grown teeth, come back to chew. At seventeen, pain found a new way in. A migraine knocked me flat. Blinding.

Nauseating. I was sprawled on the couch in the dark, whispering, “Turn the lights off.” Mom handed me a pill—hydro, something numbing. Thirty minutes later, I was at the sink, scrubbing dishes like I was reborn. “What is this?” I asked, soap dripping from my hands. “Kills pain. Feels good,” she said.

I’d never touched a damn plate before—but now I was floating. The next day, I popped one before work at Western Sizzlin. Thought, This’ll make the grind bearable. By seventeen, I was hooked.

Hydros, Tylenol 3s and 4s, Darvocets, morphine, K-pins, chewed-up fentanyl patches. Anything that numbed. Later, Roxy, straight to the vein. I wasn’t just silencing physical pain.

I was muting the constant roar inside. That fire didn’t just burn. It consumed.

Chapter 3 Pain Takes Root The discharge papers from ’99 said I was stable. I told myself I was done. Clean slate. Straight line. I believed it for about three weeks.

You don’t walk out of a psych ward healed. You walk out raw.

The alarms hadn’t reset. The wiring hadn’t changed. The only difference was I was supposed to carry it all without the chemical buffer.

Then the migraine came back.

Same blind pain. Same bargaining with the ceiling. I knew where the pills were. Told myself it was just this once. Just to function. Just to get through the shift.

“Just this once” is how dependency takes root. Not with a party. Not with a rebellion. With a Tuesday.

Ten hours on the line. Grease on my hands. Sweat in my eyes. Hydrocodone in my palm that turned the volume down from screaming to hum. I didn’t feel high. I felt normal.

That’s the real hook. Normal becomes the drug when your baseline is panic.

By nineteen, I wasn’t using to escape. I was using to operate.

Tylenol 3s, Darvocets, whatever I could source. I learned the math of maintenance:

How many milligrams get you through a double.

How to split a pill to stretch it.

How to keep your voice steady when your pupils pin and your hands shake.

I wasn’t living. I was running a machine on borrowed fuel.

From the outside, it looked like a man holding it together. I showed up. I worked. I joked at the line. I nodded through conversations. Inside, every interaction ran through chemical equilibrium.

I smiled while my nervous system ran on fumes. I made plans while my brain calculated the next dose.

Pain stopped being a warning. It became the baseline. The pills weren’t recreation. They were infrastructure.

I got good at it. Too good.

The better I performed, the deeper the dependency sank. I told myself I was in control. I wasn’t. I was pacing myself inside a cage I’d built bolt by bolt.

Every relapse wasn’t a moral failure. It was a nervous system demanding equilibrium it couldn’t generate on its own. I didn’t know the language then. I just knew the silence the pills bought, and how expensive it was getting over time.

The town felt smaller. Options thinner. I wasn’t looking for love. I was looking for an anchor—someone who could tether me to the surface when the internal current tried to drag me under.

I didn’t know it then, but I was already building the structure. I just hadn’t met the lock yet.

Then came the steakhouse.

The dish pit. The line. The bet. I thought I was playing a game—pushing speed, competing on send-backs, trying to prove something. I didn’t realize I was about to sign a twenty-year lease on my own nervous system.

I thought I was choosing a girl. I was really choosing the next place to hide.

Pain doesn’t announce itself as a life sentence. It moves in quietly. Takes up a chair. Learns your routines. By the time you realize it’s never leaving, you’re already paying rent.

And I was already packed.

Chapter 4: Twenty Years of Static "Understand: people will constantly attack you in life. One of their main weapons will be to instill in you doubts about yourself—your worth, your abilities, your potential." —Robert Greene At seventeen, a stupid bet changed everything. My cousin and I were pushing each other hard at the steakhouse—racing in the dish pit, competing on the line, counting send-backs like battle scars. One Sunday, he swaggered in talking about some girl he'd spent the weekend with.

Teenage arrogance kicked in. "I'll have her attention by Wednesday," I shot back. Twenty years, four kids, and a wrecked marriage later, it turns out I won that bet. I wish I'd lost it. At first, Trinity was just a challenge.

Something to prove. If I said I was working, I'd drive ten miles out of town to shoot hoops at some random park. She'd show up anyway. Parties, friends' houses—someone would always come get me. "She's outside.

Drunk. Determined." Three months in, she told me she was pregnant. My gut knew this wasn't love. But it was responsibility.

My kid deserved something better than what I'd grown up with. So I went into overdrive—two jobs, grinding until we had a paid-off trailer and a car with no payments. On August 10, 2002, our first child, Maverick, was born. We were still in Clarksville, trying to build a family on top of a fault line.

Less than a year later, on June 7, 2003, Shayla came. Only after Shayla was born did we move to Little Rock, where I juggled college and full-time work, trying to build something solid with two kids already here and a nervous system I didn't understand. The next twenty years ran on a loop: Break up. Crash back together.

Love twisted with wreckage. Repeat. Even when we were apart, it never stopped burning. Trinity learned where my soft spots were.

The kids became weapons. Step out of line and she'd threaten to kick me out. The thought of losing them wasn't just heartbreak. It was annihilation—another abandonment my nervous system couldn't survive.

I tried to leave. Tried to set boundaries. I always came back. For them.

Never for her. One day, my oldest daughter—maybe sixteen—looked me in the eye and asked, "Why do you let Mom be so mean to you? Why don't you find someone nicer?" I thought I'd hidden the damage. Turns out kids see everything.

I already had demons, but this relationship made me small. Any time I hurt—grief, stress, the weight of existing—it got turned back on me. You think you're suffering? Try being me.

Be a man. Most people grow up. It's all in your head. You're obsessing.

You're making something that's not there. There was no curiosity. No comfort. Just my pain reframed as selfishness.

Every time I tried to say, "I'm not okay," I ended up listening to a monologue about her stress, her struggles, her life. My feelings never got answered. They got erased. Then came the verdict: I was selfish.

I only thought about myself. You hear that long enough, it sinks into your bones. At the time, I didn't know a damn thing about Complex PTSD. I didn't know what a nervous system was or what hypervigilance meant.

All I knew was that being around people felt like too much, and my body was constantly on edge, so I called it social anxiety. I thought I was weak. Broken. Needy.

I thought the problem was me. When I relapsed, she didn't help. She punished. Kicked me out.

Weaponized the kids. I learned fast: don't bring pain here. That kind of rejection doesn't just sting. It rewires you.

Maybe that's why I drank the way I did. Or maybe that was just the lie I told myself to keep from screaming. Looking back now—with therapy and brutal self-examination behind me—I can see what I couldn't then. I saw her control, her cruelty, my weakness for staying.

I didn't see my nervous system, wired for constant alarm since infancy—from NICU trauma, childhood chaos, and a lifetime of hypervigilance. I was living with undiagnosed CPTSD and no language for why everything felt like a threat. In 2020, Trinity was working as a nurse, so I went to her doctor, a woman she trusted. Trinity told her I was bipolar.

I didn't correct it. I was hiding a meth addiction and assumed the chaos in my head was chemical, self-inflicted: mood swings, agitation, crashes. I thought it was the drugs. So I stayed quiet.

The diagnosis stuck. Bipolar. Depression. Meds stacked up—blood pressure, cholesterol, vitamin D, testosterone shots, B12, metformin.

I became a walking pharmacy, treating symptoms while the real wound kept bleeding. For years, I believed the story we were all telling: my addiction was the problem. A character flaw. Something broken in me.

I didn't know substances weren't the disease. They were my desperate attempt to regulate a nervous system stuck in constant alarm. When those outside labels weren't enough, I went hunting for worse ones myself. I started reading about Borderline Personality Disorder.

On paper, it fit too well: emotional chaos, relationship wreckage, shame, fear of abandonment, every conflict feeling like the end of the world. Nobody was giving me a better map, so I grabbed that one. I self-diagnosed as borderline and carried it like a guilty verdict for over a year. I wasn't saying "poor me." I was saying, If this is what I am, at least now I know why I ruin everything.

Meanwhile, agoraphobia closed in like a vise. Walking outside felt like stepping into incoming fire. Going to the mailbox might as well have been walking into a riot. My chest tightened just thinking about leaving the house.

My whole system went to red alert at the door. That's when I finally reached out and got a therapist on the line. Two minutes into our first conversation, she cut through years of noise. "You don't have BPD," she said. "You have Complex PTSD. And you've been manipulated." Just like that.

One sentence, and the floor shifted. She explained that CPTSD gets mistaken for BPD all the time. From the outside, trauma-wired behavior looks like instability: outbursts, shutdowns, panic, relationship chaos. People see the explosions.

They don't see the blueprint underneath—the NICU, the violence, the losses, the twenty years of static. Under all that mess, she said, I didn't have a broken personality. I had a trauma injury. That word—manipulated—hit harder than the diagnosis.

Suddenly, my whole marriage snapped into focus: The one-upping every time I tried to talk about pain. The "you're selfish, you only think about yourself." The "be a man, most people grow up." The "it's all in your head, you're making something that's not there." I had spent years calling myself every ugly name I could think of. The truth was simpler and sharper: I'd been hurt, invalidated, and gaslit until I turned the knife on myself. Eventually, even her family tired of the chaos.

After one separation, they gave her an ultimatum: take him back or we're done helping you. She took me back—just not publicly. For a year, I stayed silent when her family called. When they visited, I hid in closets—literally becoming invisible, just like I'd learned as a kid.

She kept me blocked on Facebook, status set to single, refusing to acknowledge our marriage publicly. We were legally married with four kids. And I was hiding in closets. The Trap Snaps Shut December 2014.

The static finally broke into a siren. DHS showed up at the house. A neighbor was mad at Trinity and called them on us. We'd been fighting, so I'd stayed at a hotel for the weekend.

Sunday morning, I went back to get some clothes. We got into it on the porch. While we were arguing, a car pulled up. A guy got out, asked who I was, and said he was with DHS.

He needed me to take a drug test and show him what was in my bag. He said he was going to his car to get the cup. He was almost to his door when Trinity opened the front door and screamed, asking who the fuck I had at her house. I told her it was DHS.

Told her to go talk to him. When she went out to yell at him, I used those thirty seconds. I went into the bedroom, pulled my gun and my drugs out of my bag, and shoved them under the dresser. I put the empty bag on the bed and sat down.

They walked in. He handed me the cup. "What am I gonna find?" he asked. "Meth," I said. I took the test. He looked at the results. "It's meth and Xanax." I looked at him. "Why the fuck would I be honest about meth and lie about Xanax?

I don't take Xanax." He didn't care. He looked at me and said it would be in the kids' best interest if I left and never came back. That was the downhill. The trap snapped shut, and the system was designed so I couldn't win.

Here is how it works: if one parent loses the kids but the other parent keeps them, the losing parent gets zero visitation. If we both lost them, I would have had a shot. But Trinity kept them. So I was locked out completely.

I couldn't be around my kids for a year. We split up. I was talking to a girl from Conway. I had my bags packed, just waiting on her to come get me.

That's when my wife and my mom tag-teamed me. They got me court-ordered into rehab. They thought if I was clean and sober, I wouldn't go through with the split. I did my twenty-one days.

I was pissed. I got sent off over a woman I wasn't even with. When I got out, I was laying in bed at my mom's house. The phone rang.

It was Chastidy. She asked if I wanted to go watch a movie. Twenty minutes into that movie, that's how we got together. The Longest Twenty-Four Hours It wasn't a grand romance.

It was just the next room to hide in after the last one burned down. But I got attached to Chastidy. Bad. The kind of attachment where you can't let go, even when the floor is giving way beneath you.

When she finally left me to go back to her husband, Jared, I was shipwrecked. Four days later, I drove out to their place. Jared was a good friend of mine, and we sat there and talked for hours. I went to their house that afternoon, and I didn't end up leaving for a year and a half.

They gave me my own room. The house was way out in the sticks. Out there, if you heard a car coming down the road, you knew it was coming for that house. I didn't have a word for hypervigilance back then; I didn't understand why my brain was always scanning the woodline.

But the truth was, I felt calmer out there. I felt safe. I felt safe because of Jared. One day, we were riding in the car.

Jared's mom was driving, he was in the front passenger seat, and I was in the back. Some local guy—a dude three times Jared's size—had been threatening me. Jared didn't hesitate. He called the guy right then and there from the car.

I had never heard anyone get that loud in a closed vehicle before. Jared started absolutely chewing his ass, laying into him without an ounce of fear. The dude got spooked and hung up on him. Jared called right back.

The guy's girlfriend answered the phone this time. She told Jared that the dude had literally gone and hid in the closet because he thought Jared was already on his way to the house. For the first time in my life, I felt protected. I felt like somebody else was willing to stand between me and the storm so I could finally let my guard down.

At the time, I didn't understand that dynamic. I just beat myself up and told myself I was a pussy for relying on it. But my body knew what it was doing. It was staying where the shield was.

Trinity knew it, too. She watched the whole thing from the outside and figured out exactly how to rig the trap. Chastidy started suspecting that Jared and I were on drugs. Trinity saw her opening.

She went out and bought the drug tests herself and gave them to Chastidy, knowing damn well that if I failed, Chastidy would kick me out and I'd have nowhere left to run but straight back to Trinity's house. The plan worked. I took the test, and Chastidy gave me twenty-four hours to pack my shit and get out. Those were the longest, hardest twenty-four hours of my life.

Stripping that safety away felt like being skinned alive. Trinity came and picked me up, but I refused to give her the satisfaction. The second we pulled up to her house, I had a friend waiting in the driveway to pick me up and carry me away. I wasn't going back into that cage.

Four days passed. Jared called me up and told me Chastidy was at work, asking if I wanted to come out and hang out with him. I went. About an hour before she was scheduled to get off work, Jared looked at me and asked if I just wanted to stay the night. "Dude," I said. "She just kicked me out four days ago.

She's not gonna let me stay here." Jared called her anyway. To my surprise, she said yes. When we got back to the trailer, the reality hit. Chastidy told me she was converting my old bedroom for her daughter.

It wasn't my room anymore. It was deep winter, freezing cold, and that trailer had zero central heat. The only warmth in the entire place was a small space heater running in their bedroom. Chastidy pointed to a recliner sitting right next to their bed.

In front of the bed, there was a dresser with a TV and a stereo on it. She said I could sleep in the recliner. So I did. For the next six to nine months, I lived in that recliner.

I slept right next to their bed every single night, with the TV and stereo on, just to stay near the only heat and the only protection I had left in the world. "I'm tired of raising you like a child," she'd say, driving the shame deeper. My fawn response, perfected early, convinced me erasing myself was the only way to keep any kind of peace. Distrusting my own perception made her gaslighting—intentional or not—brutally effective. Those moments in closets weren't a metaphor someone cooked up later.

They were literal. Proof that my existence felt like something that needed to be managed. The marriage didn't create my trauma. It trained it.

Refined it. Confirmed it. Every time I was told I was selfish, crazy, too much, weak, or impossible, something old in me nodded and said, Exactly. That's what I've been trying to tell you.

By the end, the worst thing the marriage took wasn't peace. It was trust in my own reality. And when a man stops trusting his own reality, he becomes very easy to steer.

Chapter 5 The Collapse By 2020, the fire had eaten everything.

Trinity took the kids—Maverick, Shayla, Teaguen, Adalee—and left the four-bedroom house hollow. I couldn’t afford it alone. Didn’t need the space anyway. I moved in with a buddy, mattress on his floor, my life compressed down to whatever fit beside it. The meth pipe stayed within reach. My skin crawled. My teeth chattered. Smoke kept me upright—barely.

What started around September 2020 hardened into a pattern: daily use, broken only by short, fragile attempts at sobriety that never lasted more than a couple of weeks. I hadn’t taken psychiatric medication since before COVID. I quit all of it abruptly, around the same time everything else fell apart. I told myself I was managing.

In December 2021, reality punched through whatever illusion I had left.

Mom was in the living room. I was in my old bedroom with friends—laughter sharp, meth humming through the walls.

“Guys, I don’t feel right,” she called out. Quiet at first. Then louder.

“I’m dizzy. I’m gonna fall.”

Falon moved fast. Door flying open. Shouting for help.

And I froze.

Not metaphorically. Not emotionally. Physically. Exactly like I had under that blanket as a kid. My body locked while my mind went blank.

People yelling my name.

“Kali, move.”

I couldn’t.

Mom was on the floor. Falon grabbed her. Tina called 911. The living room filled with paramedics.

“Who am I?” my sister asked.

“Paula,” Mom said—naming her dead sister.

Her blood sugar was over 600.

“She won’t make it,” someone said.

Three days later, her heart stopped in her sleep.

At the visitation, Walter drove. Truck door opened. The crowd closed in. I choked—sobs stuck somewhere behind my ribs.

On the day of the funeral, I stayed in her house alone, her perfume still clinging to a scarf. Walter called.

“Where are you?”

“Not going,” I said.

His sister dragged me anyway. We got to the graveside mid-service. Thirty yards from the casket, I stopped. Dirt still raw. Voices low.

They told me to sit up front.

“No.”

My boots stayed planted. Meth buzzing. Too far gone to feel it right. My brother was dead at thirty-two. Now my mom. Loss stacked on loss until I couldn’t tell where grief ended and numbness began.

I’d tried rehab before. In 2019, I walked out after two weeks. My first call was from Trinity—bills, kids, stress. I thought leaving was what she wanted. Later she said it wasn’t.

In 2024, I checked myself in again. Three days later, I was medically discharged. Sleep apnea. No CPAP. Another failed attempt.

By then, I wasn’t fighting to get better. I was fighting to stay conscious.

The collapse wasn’t dramatic. It wasn’t loud. It was quiet, structural, and complete. Everything that had been holding me upright was gone.

Chapter 6 The Breaking Point (Garrett’s Loss)

Let me take you back to 2011.

The outside storm of my childhood had mostly passed, but the fire inside me was still burning. Years of pain, avoidance, and drug use had kept it alive. From the outside, my life looked stable. Inside, I was barely holding together. I had a house. Kids. A marriage. A life that looked contained. But underneath all of that, I was surviving on avoidance and weak compromises. I was numb most of the time, and I had gotten good at pretending that was normal.

The last time I saw him Thursday, June 23, 2011.

I was at my parents’ house, sitting on the toilet, trying to take a shit, when someone started pounding on the door.

“Open up!” Garrett yelled.

“I’m taking a shit!” I snapped back.

The pounding got louder.

“Open the door, man. You’ll be happy. I’ve got a surprise for you.”

I opened the door, still half-seated on the toilet.

Garrett stood there with that intense look in his eyes. There was something frantic behind it, something wild and urgent. Before I could really react, he grabbed my arm, found a vein, and slid a needle in. Just as fast, he pulled it out.

“See?” he said.

Then he walked out of the bathroom. Out of the house.

That was the last time I ever saw him alive.

Garrett

His full name was William Garrett Lingar.

He was thirty-two. Wild. Restless. Always looking over his shoulder. There were warrants. Chaos. Late nights. Bad choices. Fear hiding under bravado. He was a mirror I didn’t want to look into.

I envied his freedom. No real ties. No house. No marriage. No kids depending on him. He could move however he wanted—or at least that’s how it looked to me then.

He envied what I had. The kids. The house. The structure. A life that looked like it had a frame around it.

We used to drink beer and talk about being jealous of each other.

Looking back now, as a grown man living more like he lived back then, I see it differently.

I had it all.

I just couldn’t feel it. I was zombified—walking through a life I should have been grateful for, too numb and buried in myself to understand what I had. If God allowed redos, I’d go back to being nineteen or twenty. I’d love every waking, zombified moment. I’d hold my kids tighter. I’d pay attention. I’d stop thinking life was something waiting for me somewhere else.

I envy that younger version of me now.

He had more than he knew.

Kansas City Friday, June 24, 2011.

Arkansas summer heat pushed thick against everything. Greg’s mom picked me up in Clarksville and drove me to Fayetteville to meet Greg. From there, just me and Greg headed to Kansas City to help move Beth back to Arkansas. There was a U-Haul. A house waiting in Goshen. Heavy work to do.

Just me and Greg.

A week earlier, Garrett had talked to Greg and said he’d go help in Kansas City. I knew that. But that morning, I didn’t wake Garrett up before I left. I didn’t go back to his room. I didn’t knock on his door.

In the moment, it felt like nothing. Just a small decision. A non-event. One of those choices you make without understanding that your whole life may circle back to it later.

I would replay that decision for the rest of my life.

Halfway up the highway, my phone rang. It was Garrett.

His voice was pure rage.

“What the fuck, dude? Greg’s a cop. I would’ve ridden with him. You knew I wanted to go!”

Before I could answer, the line went dead.

The wreck

Later, the pieces came together.

After we left, Garrett took my dad’s Xterra and drove to his girlfriend’s place looking for his meds. She said she didn’t have them. He called the cops. Everything escalated. Then she called them back and accused him of rape. They questioned him for hours. Then they let him go.

By then, he was unraveling.

That night, he bought beer. He drank. Then he drove.

On Brick Plant Road, he lost control. Ninety miles an hour into a tree. The impact was so violent it threw the headlights fifty yards from the wreck.

Garrett died instantly.

The phone call in Goshen

Saturday morning in Kansas City felt normal until it didn’t.

I was sitting in the U-Haul when Greg came out and told me I had a phone call. I followed him into the house. Someone handed me the phone.

It was Trinity.

“Garrett’s gone,” she said. “He’s dead. There was a wreck.”

The world stopped.

I don’t remember whether I dropped the phone or handed it back. That part is gone. I remember going outside. I walked about thirty yards into the yard. Then I collapsed. I punched the ground because I needed something solid to hit. I looked up at the sky and screamed, “Fuck you, God. Fuck you.”

Beth’s mom must have seen me in the yard. Maybe she heard me cussing God. She was the first person I remember seeing after the call.

I didn’t have thoughts. It felt like I had just been born into pain—mind wiped clean, all that was left this one impossible thing.

My brother was dead.

She opened her arms.

And I lost it.

She walked me over to the porch swing and sat down with me. I folded toward her like a little kid. My head ended up in her lap, and I cried like a two-year-old. Loud. Messy. Snot and tears everywhere. All over her.

She didn’t pull away. She didn’t tell me to calm down. She didn’t make a speech. She didn’t try to fix it.

She just sat there and rocked me.

I didn’t have words for it then. Now I do: she lent me her nervous system until mine could breathe again.

It may have been the kindest thing anyone ever did for me. It was also the first time my emotions outweighed my social anxiety. For once, I wasn’t worried about being seen.

I was too broken to hide.

The verdict

The guilt came right away.

Not as a question. As a verdict.

If I would have woken him up. If I had knocked on his door. If I hadn’t left without him.

He would have had different choices in front of him. Maybe he would have gone to Kansas City. Maybe he would have ridden with us. Maybe he wouldn’t have gone to that house looking for his meds. Maybe he wouldn’t have been questioned for hours. Maybe he wouldn’t have bought the beer. Maybe he wouldn’t have been on Brick Plant Road that night.

That’s how guilt works. It doesn’t need the whole chain. It finds one small link and hangs your whole life from it.

None of it felt like clean grief.

It felt like proof.

Proof that I was dangerous to the people I loved. Proof that being close to me could get people hurt. Proof that I had failed him.

My cousin offered to take me home right then. I said no. On the surface, it was about finishing the move. The deeper truth was simpler:

I didn’t want to go home.

Some part of me believed that if I stayed in Kansas City, maybe it hadn’t fully happened yet. Maybe it wouldn’t be real until I crossed back into Arkansas.

That night, we went to the Royals and Cubs game. My first professional baseball game.

The grass was so green it felt fake. A stadium full of people. Lights bright. Sound loud. Life roaring around a man who had just been split open.

I remember looking at the field and thinking: How can the world be this alive when my brother is dead?

The game went on.

So did I.

But that weekend in Kansas City was the point where the wiring finally snapped tight around one idea:

I was the common denominator in too much wreckage.

And from there, every loss had a new echo:

This is your fault.

Chapter 7

Adrift in North Dakota

After Garrett died, the unraveling didn’t happen fast. It stretched. Frayed. Like a rope taking weight it was never meant to carry. By the time my marriage finally collapsed in 2020, the fire had already been burning for years.

The opiate addiction that started when I was seventeen had changed shape around 2015. Pills gave way to Suboxone for six months—just long enough to convince me I was stabilizing—before meth took over. Sometimes injected. Sometimes traded for alcohol. One master replacing another. The illusion of choice masking the same captivity.

When Trinity took the kids, the house emptied out in a way that felt physical. Four bedrooms reduced to echoes. I couldn’t afford it alone. Didn’t need the space anyway. I slept on a buddy’s floor for a while, my life compressed down to whatever could fit beside a mattress and a pipe.

I needed distance. A reset. Somewhere far enough away that the noise might finally stop.

So I went north.

Williston, North Dakota. Full-time work at Love’s Truck Stop. Cold air. Long hours. Structure. I told myself it was a fresh start. What I didn’t understand yet was that you can’t outrun a nervous system. You just give it new terrain. The fire came with me.

Work became the next addiction. It was cleaner. Legal. Praised. I threw myself into it with the same compulsive intensity I’d always used to survive. Maybe I wouldn’t feel the ache of my kids being gone.

For the first six months, they thought I was a miracle. I worked like five people. Learned every position. Took every extra task. Unloaded trucks—dry goods, walk-in, deep freeze. Stocked everything. Cleaned what no one else wanted to touch. I trained new hires not because I was promoted, but because I’d absorbed everything through sheer, compulsive effort.

The praise came fast. The raises followed.

For a while, it felt like proof that this time was different.

It never is.

Burnout doesn’t announce itself. It creeps in quietly. One morning, I was scheduled for a 5 a.m. shift. I woke to pounding on my apartment window. Sunlight everywhere. It was 9 a.m. Shame hit like a gut punch. I swore it wouldn’t happen again.

The next morning, it did. Same pounding. Same time. Same humiliation.

A week later, I realized my alarm volume had somehow been set to mute. A simple explanation. Too late to matter. I was taken off mornings permanently and put on straight second shifts.

That created its own hell.

We were perpetually short-staffed. For four solid months, I closed both restaurants alone. Every dish from two kitchens. Sweeping and mopping massive floors. Hauling trash. Cleaning three deep fryers. Boxing leftover food. Doing prep for the next day. Sometimes it was two in the morning before I left, reeking of grease and exhaustion.

Even then, boundaries didn’t exist. The night manager from the store side—someone I didn’t even technically work for—would wander back, see me drowning, and ask me to do extra tasks for him. Coffee. Cleaning. Whatever he didn’t want to deal with. I couldn’t say no. The best I could manage was to forget.

Social anxiety wrapped tighter the longer I stayed. Walking out of my apartment felt like stepping onto a stage. Every glance felt like judgment. I walked the entire perimeter of the building just to enter through the back, avoiding the front entrance. I took tasks in the back whenever I could. Customer interaction drained me. Fluorescent lights buzzed like they were inside my skull.

I was constantly late—ten minutes, fifteen, sometimes thirty. Not because I didn’t care, but because there was a war in my chest before every shift. The only jobs where it never happened were the ones where I was in charge.

When I wasn’t working, I disappeared. I avoided stores unless it was an emergency. If Love’s didn’t stock what I needed, I paid someone else to go to Walmart. When I did go myself, it was at two or three in the morning. Less people. Less threat.

The move north didn’t extinguish the fire. It isolated it. I was surrounded by people and completely alone. Sixteen-hour days and solitary closing shifts didn’t silence grief or heal loss. They just proved what I’d been running from my whole life.

The storm wasn’t outside.

Chapter 8: Anatomy of a Spiral Trinity, Tina, Kristina — Dissecting the Damage This wasn’t three love stories. It was one wound wearing three different faces. For years, I kept those names in separate boxes so I wouldn’t have to look at the pattern straight on. Trinity was the marriage.

Tina was the escape. Kristina was the mind game. Three different women. Three different disasters.

Three different explanations for why everything in my life kept catching fire. That version was easier to live with because it let me keep pretending each wreck was self-contained. If every collapse belonged to a different room, then maybe there was no architecture connecting them. Maybe I was just unlucky.

Maybe the whole thing was random. It wasn’t random. The women were different. The dynamics were different.

The damage took different shapes. But the wound underneath was the same. Every one of those relationships plugged into the same circuitry: abandonment, shame, panic, self-erasure, and the deep animal terror that connection could disappear at any second. I did not move from one relationship to the next because I was learning.

I moved because I was repeating. I was carrying the same raw wiring into different rooms and calling each fire by a different name. This chapter is not a blame list. It is not a courtroom.

It is not a plea for sympathy. It is an autopsy. Trinity: The Training Ground Trinity was not the beginning of my damage, but she became the longest environment my damage ever lived in. When I met her, I was still young enough to confuse responsibility with readiness.

A pregnancy came fast, and the part of me that had grown up around chaos latched onto the idea that I could outrun my own instability by building something solid on the outside. Work hard. Pay bills. Get the trailer.

Get the car. Show up. Be a dad. Be useful.

If the frame looked stable enough, maybe nobody would notice the fire inside it. From the outside, it probably looked like a life. Inside it, the static never stopped. The deeper damage didn’t come from the fighting.

It came from what happened to my sense of reality inside it. Any time I tried to bring pain into the room, the room turned on me. Grief became selfishness. Confusion became weakness.

Panic became immaturity. If I said I was drowning, I got handed a speech about how someone else had it worse. That kind of invalidation is not just hurtful. It is instructional.

It teaches you which parts of yourself are unacceptable. It teaches you to scan your own feelings before you speak and ask not, “Is this true?” but, “Will this get used against me?” Over time, that question becomes your real personality. I began editing myself before I even spoke. I started shrinking in real time.

I learned how to swallow reactions, delay needs, soften truths, and disappear inside conversations that were supposed to be intimate. The safest version of me became the least visible one. And the kids made that system almost impossible to leave. People look at the marriage from the outside and ask why I stayed.

They assume I stayed because I believed in the relationship. The truth is uglier and more nervous-system-driven than that. I stayed because my connection to my children had become braided into the relationship’s control structure. The thought of losing them did not feel like heartbreak.

It felt like annihilation. Every threat, every separation, every hint that access to my kids could be restricted hit a place in me that was older than reason. It was body terror. The most humiliating symbol of that whole period was also the most honest.

I hid in closets. Not metaphorically. Literally. There were times I stayed out of sight in my own life because being acknowledged felt more dangerous than being erased.

Her family might come around and I would make myself invisible. She could keep me blocked publicly, keep the image she wanted intact, while I physically shrank into whatever hidden space was available. We were married. We had children together.

And I was hiding like my existence needed to be managed. That is not just dysfunction. That is embodiment. A child who once learned that survival meant being still had grown into a man who could disappear on command if he thought it would preserve attachment.

The closet was not a symbol I invented later. It was a room my nervous system recognized immediately. The marriage did not create my trauma. But it trained it.

Refined it. Confirmed it. By the end, the worst thing the marriage had taken from me was not peace. It was trust in my own perceptions.

And when a man stops trusting his own perceptions, he becomes easy to steer. Tina: The Starvation Hook By the time Tina entered the picture, I was not making decisions from stability. I was making them from collapse. People talk about love like it happens on neutral ground.

But a lot of what gets called love does not begin in clarity. Sometimes it begins in emergency. Sometimes it begins when somebody has been starving so long that relief itself starts to feel sacred. That was the condition I was in.

I was carrying years of shame, an addiction that had become a regulator, and the growing terror of losing my family structure. I was not looking at the world with a steady nervous system and asking who was healthy for me. I was gasping for something that did not feel like drowning. When a person enters your life at that exact point, your body does not calmly assess them.

It lunges. That is what Tina became to me. Not because she was magic. But because she arrived in the middle of collapse, and collapse changes the meaning of attention.

A person who makes room for you when you feel erased can look like salvation. A person who listens when you feel disbelieved can look like destiny. When you are emotionally starving, relief can feel like love. I didn’t bond from steadiness.

I bonded from panic. The part of me attaching was not the calm adult evaluating what a relationship could actually hold. It was the abandoned part. The flooded part.

The part that believed one more loss might split me open for good. That kind of attachment always carries distortion with it. Attention gets overread. Availability gets romanticized.

Relief gets mistaken for compatibility. The body says, “I can breathe here,” and the mind rushes to build a future around that first exhale. I made Tina larger than reality because I needed her to be larger than reality. I needed the connection to mean something big enough to justify the risk, the disloyalty, the rupture.

I needed the story to be rescue, because if it wasn’t rescue, then maybe it was just another symptom of how desperate I had become. Desperation can make a person feel profound. Not because the feelings are fake, but because urgency amplifies everything. A starving nervous system is not a reliable narrator.

It can take a little bit of warmth and turn it into revelation. I don’t say that to cheapen what Tina was. I say it to tell the truth about what I was. I was not choosing from freedom.

I was choosing from fear. And fear, when it is dressed up like hope, can be one of the most convincing forces in the world. Kristina: The Hall of Mirrors Kristina hit different because she landed where my mind was weakest. If Trinity had trained me to doubt myself, and Tina had met me in raw need, Kristina stepped into the territory where being seen and being studied started to blur.

What pulled me in first was recognition. She seemed to understand things that mattered to me. Reflected interests back at me. Mirrored emotional language in ways that made the connection feel unusually specific.

For a person who had spent years feeling erased, that kind of resonance can feel supernatural. But being mirrored is not always the same thing as being known. Sometimes it is access. The parts of me most hungry to be recognized were the same parts most vulnerable to manipulation.

If somebody touched that wound the right way, they did not just get my attention. They got my hunger. And hunger is easy to guide. Gaslighting works best when the ground is already unstable.

By the time that relationship formed, I had years of self-doubt behind me. Years of being told I was overreacting. Years of second-guessing what I felt. So when contradictions showed up, when signals got crossed, I did not anchor myself in my own experience.

I folded inward and went hunting for what was wrong with me. That is how a person can end up trapped in confusion that looks invisible from the outside. You replay conversations. Reconstruct tone.

Analyze pauses. You stop asking, “Is this treatment acceptable?” and start asking, “Why am I so affected by this?” That is a devastating shift because it relocates the whole problem into your own mind while the dynamic keeps cutting you. The relationship became a hall of mirrors. I could not tell whether I was seeing clearly or projecting.

I kept returning to the very dynamic that was intensifying the confusion in hopes that it would also provide clarity. That is not connection. That is captivity with emotional language wrapped around it. But I was not passive in it.

I brought my own fire into that room. I brought obsession, projection, savior-hunger, abandonment terror, and the impossible demand that another person help me organize pain that had started decades before they arrived. No relationship can safely carry that much historical weight. When it tries, the structure warps.

Kristina did not create the belief that I was fundamentally broken. She touched it. That was enough. Once that belief gets activated, everything grows larger than the facts.

The present gets drowned by all the rooms it accidentally opened. The Spiral For a long time, I used these women like evidence in a trial. See? That’s why I used.

That’s why I ran. That’s why I broke. But if I stop the story there, then all I have done is rename my suffering and hand the power for it entirely to other people. The more useful truth is harder and less flattering.

The common denominator was me. Not because I was the villain in every room. But because I carried the same unhealed wiring into each relationship and then acted shocked when it produced familiar fires. That wiring had rules: When I felt unseen, I latched onto whoever made me feel recognized.

When I felt threatened with abandonment, I reached like separation might kill me. When I got confused, I assumed I was defective. When shame flared, I hid. When panic rose past what my body could regulate, I used.

When using created more shame, I hid harder. That is the spiral. Trinity, Tina, and Kristina were not separate detours from the same life. They were three expressions of the same unresolved system trying, failing, and trying again to solve pain through attachment.

What made the spiral so destructive was not just who entered these rooms, but what was already waiting inside them. Old trauma, shame, invalidation, and unregulated fear turned each connection into a test it was never built to pass. For most of my life, I only knew how to think in extremes: it is all their fault or it is all mine. Neither one gets close enough to the truth to be useful.

The truth is that I was wounded and responsible. Manipulated in some places and destructive in others. Capable of being harmed and fully capable of causing harm. Reaching for love with tools built for survival.

I was not entering relationships with actual relational tools. I was entering them with freeze, fawn, shame, hypervigilance, emotional hunger, and chemical coping. Then I expected intimacy to grow out of that soil. No wonder the roots twisted.

What It Was Really Asking Under all three of those relationships, one question kept repeating itself in me, even when I did not have language for it: Can I be close to someone without disappearing? That was the real question under the marriage. Under the affair. Under the obsession.

Under the panic. Under the relapses. Under the hiding. And because I did not know that was the question, I kept answering it with the wrong equipment.

I confused intensity with depth. Relief with safety. Chemistry with rescue. Being studied with being understood.

Emotional adrenaline with love. So I kept stepping into versions of the same fire and telling myself each one was new. It wasn’t new. It was familiar.

And familiarity has a dangerous way of impersonating home when trauma has been your earliest architecture. Turning Toward the Machinery For a long time, I thought that if I understood this pattern too clearly, all it would do is confirm the ugliest things I already believed about myself. Weak. Broken.

Needy. Crazy. But that is not what happened. Worse, because I could no longer hide behind randomness.

I had to see the repetition. I had to admit I was carrying tinder into every room. Better, because the pattern gave me something I had almost never had before: a map. A map does not erase damage.

It does not undo betrayals. It does not return lost years, lost trust, or lost versions of yourself. But it does let you stop wandering blind inside the same wreckage. Once I could see that the spiral was not random, I could stop spending all my energy asking who struck the match and start asking what in me kept making the rooms so flammable.

That question changed everything. Not all at once. Not cleanly. Not enough to save what had already burned.

But enough to turn me toward the machinery underneath the story. Seeing the spiral was only the first step. Naming the pattern didn’t fix it, but it stopped me from pretending every collapse was random. The next step wasn’t finding better people or begging the past to change.

It was turning toward the machinery underneath it all—the blueprint, the wiring, the way my nervous system had been set to danger long before any of these relationships showed up. That’s where the fire really started.

Chapter 09 — The Wrong Diagnoses

Part Four: Decoding the Pattern

If I could shift the blame off myself, who was I supposed to give it to instead? The system felt like the answer. But the system had its own answer ready.

When everything finally collapsed, people needed a name for it.

So did I.

Doctors listened in fragments. Ten minutes. Checklists. Symptoms pulled out of context and lined up like evidence. Mood swings. Substance use. Insomnia. Impulsivity. Depression. Anxiety. Anger.

They didn't ask what came first.

They didn't ask what my body had learned before my mind ever had a chance.

They gave me labels instead.

Bipolar. Major depressive disorder. Generalized anxiety.

Each diagnosis came with a prescription. Each prescription came with side effects. Some flattened me. Some agitated me. Some made the fire burn hotter instead of cooler. When they didn't work, the assumption wasn't that the map was wrong—it was that I wasn't compliant enough.

I tried harder.

I swallowed pills on schedules I didn't believe in. I tracked moods I didn't understand. I learned how to describe my pain in ways that fit intake forms. I became fluent in pathology.

None of it made me safer.

The problem with the diagnoses wasn't that they were malicious. It was that they were incomplete. They described what I did when overwhelmed, not why overwhelm was constant. They named behaviors without naming origins.

They treated my reactions as malfunctions instead of adaptations.

No one asked about the glass box. No one asked about the house that shook at night. No one asked about freezing, hiding, staying quiet to survive.

They saw fire and assumed the spark was internal.

I started believing them.

I thought I was unstable. Broken. Chemically defective. That if I could just find the right combination of medications, I'd finally become normal—whatever that meant. Every failed treatment reinforced the same story: I'm the problem.

But something didn't add up.

Even at my worst, my reactions made sense to me. They weren't random. They followed patterns. Stress led to collapse. Closeness led to panic. Silence led to numbness. Loss led to shutdown.

That isn't chaos.

That's a system doing exactly what it was built to do.

The crack came when someone finally asked a different question.

"What happened to you?"

Not what symptoms do you have. Not how intense are they.

What happened.

I didn't have an answer ready. The story came out sideways. Pieces. The NICU. The house. The running. The hiding. The losses. The way my body never stood down even when life looked stable on paper.

For the first time, my past wasn't treated like background noise.

It was treated like the blueprint.

Trauma doesn't look like one thing. It doesn't announce itself cleanly. Sometimes it looks like anger. Sometimes like withdrawal. Sometimes like addiction. Sometimes like competence pushed too far.

Misdiagnosis isn't just incorrect labeling.

It's a missed opportunity to understand why the fire exists at all.

The problem wasn't that I had too much emotion.

It was that my system had been living in emergency mode for decades without relief.

The diagnoses weren't wrong.

They just weren't deep enough.

And for the first time, I started to suspect that what I'd been fighting wasn't a broken brain—

—but a body that never learned it was safe.

Chapter 10: The Glass Box Blueprint Ground zero. September 1982. Davenport, Iowa. They ripped me out too soon.

Three pounds, four ounces dropped into a glass box. This chapter isn’t just about remembering the start. It’s about exploring how that beginning—being born Very Low Birth Weight (VLBW) and spending thirty days in a 1982 Neonatal Intensive Care Unit—laid the faulty wiring for everything that followed. This is where personal reflection meets the science.

Back then, survival was the main goal. Pain management was minimal. Neonatal surgeries often occurred with little to no anesthesia until the late 1980s. Parental involvement was limited.

The environment was bright, noisy, chaotic—a far cry from the womb-like settings aimed for now. My first world wasn’t arms and heartbeats. It was acrylic walls, alarms, and distance. I don’t have conscious memories, obviously.

But my body kept score. That tightness when someone gets close, the hum under fluorescent lights, the persistent feeling of watching life through glass—it all points back. She listened without interrupting me.

When I finished, she looked at me for a long moment.

"I know why you think that," she said quietly. "I really do."

Then she told me she was telling me the truth.

She didn't become defensive. She didn't yell. She didn't try to shame me for asking.

She simply answered.

It should have settled the question. It didn't. Not for me.

Some wounds don't come from knowing too little. They come from never being able to know enough.

I believed then—and a part of me still believes now—that she was carrying my daughter. I can't prove that. I'm not asking anyone reading this to accept it as fact. This isn't a courtroom. It's a memory.

I'm telling you what lived inside my heart that night.

Whether I was right or wrong, the possibility changed everything. The woman sitting beside me wasn't just the only person who showed up. She might have been carrying my child.

That thought made the night heavier than words can explain.

There was love in it. There was grief in it. There was hope. There was confusion. There was fear. None of those feelings canceled the others out. They all existed at once. Life is rarely clean enough to separate them.

The conversation drifted after that. Sometimes we'd talk for fifteen minutes without stopping. Sometimes we'd sit in silence even longer. Neither of us seemed uncomfortable with it.

Silence feels different when someone chooses to stay.

I don't remember every word we spoke. Trauma doesn't preserve conversations. It preserves moments.

I remember the blanket wrapped around both of us. I remember her leaning back against the cab of the truck. I remember the pillow under her. I remember reaching over once to straighten the blanket because it had slipped off her shoulder. I remember the smell of fresh-cut grass mixing with warm summer air. I remember looking toward the road again and again, expecting my life to explode all over again. I remember her voice cutting through the panic whenever my mind started running too far ahead.

"You don't know what's going to happen."

She kept bringing me back to the present. One sentence at a time. One breath at a time.

She never promised everything would be okay. She never told me not to worry. She never gave me false hope.

She just stayed with me inside the uncertainty.

Looking back now, I understand something I couldn't have understood then. She wasn't solving my problems. She was helping me survive them.

At the time, I didn't know words like co-regulation. I didn't know how one calm nervous system can quietly help another one stop drowning. I only knew that somewhere between midnight and sunrise, my breathing slowed. The panic loosened its grip. The thoughts that had been screaming inside my head became whispers.

Nothing outside of me had changed. The investigation hadn't disappeared. The rumors were still spreading. The people who ignored my calls still hadn't called back. The questions about the future were still unanswered. The questions about the baby were still unanswered.

Everything that had terrified me when the sun went down was still waiting for me.

But something inside me had changed.

I wasn't carrying it by myself anymore.

That made all the difference.

Hours slipped by without either of us noticing. The darkness slowly began to soften. The black sky faded into charcoal. Then into gray. A thin line of pale orange appeared across the horizon. Birds started singing before either of us realized morning had come.

I looked over at her.

She was still there.

She had stayed the entire night. She had watched the darkness leave with me.

For most people, that might not sound extraordinary. For someone whose entire life had taught him that people disappear when things become difficult... it changed something I didn't even know could change.

For the first time in a very long time, my body had different evidence.

Not everyone leaves.

At least one person came. And when she had every reason to go home...

She stayed.

## Part 3 — The Evidence That Stayed

For years, I thought what saved me that night was the conversation.

It wasn't.

I can't tell you every word we said because I don't remember them. Trauma doesn't archive dialogue the way it archives danger. It remembers the feeling of a room, the sound of a voice, the way your chest tightens, the smell of the air. It remembers what mattered to survival.

What survived that night wasn't a conversation. It was a person.

It was the fact that when every piece of evidence in my life pointed toward abandonment, one person quietly introduced a new piece of evidence.

She came.

That sounds simple. It wasn't.

Every nervous system builds predictions from experience. Mine had been collecting the same data since the beginning.

Distance. Loss. Goodbyes. People leaving. Love that disappeared. Relationships that ended without warning.

Every experience taught my body the same lesson: don't expect anyone to stay when life gets hard.

The night on that tailgate challenged all of it. Not with an argument. With presence.

Nobody debated my trauma into healing. Nobody talked me out of my fear. Nobody convinced me to think positively.

One exhausted woman sat beside me until sunrise because she refused to let me be alone.

Sometimes that is what healing looks like. It isn't dramatic. It doesn't announce itself. It doesn't even feel like healing while it's happening.

Sometimes healing is simply discovering that one person stayed when your entire history told you no one would.

I didn't fully understand that until years later.

Life carried us in different directions. Like so many people who mattered to me, time created distance. We weren't part of each other's everyday lives anymore. There were years we barely spoke.

Then, while I was living in North Dakota, I found her again on Facebook.

It started with a simple message. Before long, it felt like no time had passed at all.

One night, I called her because I couldn't make sense of what was happening inside me.

I was on my way to another woman's house. On paper, it should have been a fresh start. My last relationship had ended more than a year earlier. I'd spent months telling myself I was ready to move on.

But as I drove there, I couldn't stop crying.

Not polite tears. Not a few drops I could wipe away and pretend were allergies. The kind of crying that comes from somewhere beneath language.

I couldn't understand it.

I remember saying, "I don't know what's wrong with me."

She was quiet for a second. Then she answered with the kind of certainty that only comes from someone who truly knows you.

"Your body's telling you this is really the end."

I didn't say anything.

She kept talking.

"You've been grieving every day since she left. Somewhere inside, you've still believed there was a chance she'd come back. Walking through that door means you're finally letting go."

The words hit me harder than I expected.

Because she was right.

The tears weren't about the woman I was going to see. They were about the woman I still hadn't released.

My mind had accepted the breakup months earlier. I hadn't.

That was something Chastidy understood before I did.

She didn't make me feel weak. She didn't tell me to "man up." She didn't tell me to get over it.

She simply recognized what my body had been trying to say.

Then she told me something I'll never forget.

"I'll stay by my phone tonight."

Those words took me back years without either of us mentioning the tailgate.

She had done it again.

So I went.

Throughout the night, I texted her. Every emotion. Every doubt. Every awkward moment. Every wave of panic. Every time I wanted to get in my truck and leave.

She answered. She never judged me. She never laughed at me. She never made me feel ridiculous for experiencing emotions that most people probably thought I should have been over.

She listened. Sometimes she answered with long messages. Sometimes just a sentence. Sometimes she only reminded me to breathe.

But she stayed.

By the time the night was over, I felt something I hadn't expected to feel.

Peace.

Not because every question had been answered. Not because my past suddenly stopped hurting. Because someone who knew the worst parts of me still believed I deserved something better than the life I'd been settling for.

Before we hung up, she said something I've carried with me ever since.

"You deserve a good woman."

Simple words. Most people would probably forget hearing them.

I couldn't.

Because I believed she meant them.

She knew the whole story. Not the polished version. Not the version people tell after they've figured everything out.

She knew about the addiction. The failures. The fear. The trauma. The mistakes. The shame. She knew the parts I usually tried to hide.

And after seeing all of it...

She still believed I deserved to be loved well.

There are compliments you remember because they flatter your ego. Then there are words that become part of your foundation because they arrive from someone who has earned the right to speak into your life.

Those words became part of mine.

People often ask what helps someone heal from Complex PTSD. They expect a complicated answer.

Sometimes there is one.

Therapy helped. Education helped. Understanding the nervous system helped. Learning the language of trauma helped.

But none of those things mattered very much until I had evidence that contradicted the story my body had believed my entire life.

Evidence. Not theory. Evidence.

One person stayed.

Then she stayed again.

Years apart. Different states. Different chapters of our lives. Different kinds of pain.

The pattern never changed. When I reached for her...

She answered.

Looking back now, I realize Chastidy never tried to become my hero. She never tried to rescue me. She never made herself the center of my story.

She simply kept showing up.

There is extraordinary power in ordinary faithfulness. Not grand gestures. Not dramatic speeches. Just showing up. Again. And again. And again.

By then, I had begun studying trauma more seriously. I understood attachment. I understood abandonment. I understood nervous system regulation. I understood why my body reacted the way it did.

But all of those ideas eventually led me back to the same place.

A brown Ford Ranger. An open tailgate. A blanket. A pillow. A woman who refused to leave.

Sometimes an entire philosophy of healing can be traced back to one unforgettable night.

Mine can.

## Part 4 — She Came

On June 28, 2025, Chastidy Dodge died.

I wish that sentence weren't true.

I wish I could tell you we talked one last time and everything was okay. I wish I could tell you I got the chance to thank her.

I didn't.

The last conversation we had ended in a misunderstanding. Nothing dramatic. Nothing that seemed important at the time. Just one of those ordinary human moments you assume you'll have another chance to fix.

You don't think, "These are the last words we'll ever exchange." You think, "We'll talk again in a few days."

Life teaches you otherwise.

When I found out she was gone, my mind refused to accept it. I kept thinking about my phone. I kept expecting a message. I kept imagining that somehow there had been a mistake.

There wasn't.

The silence that followed felt different from every silence that had come before. Not because she had left me. Because death had taken away the possibility of another conversation.

There would never be another apology. Another explanation. Another chance to say thank you. Another sunrise.

That is a particular kind of grief. It isn't just missing someone. It's mourning every conversation you'll never get to have.

For a long time, I carried guilt over that last misunderstanding. I replayed it. I rewrote it. I imagined different endings. I imagined different words. As if I could somehow negotiate with memory.

Grief does that. It convinces you there must have been one sentence capable of changing the ending.

There wasn't. Death doesn't bargain.

Eventually I realized something.

If Chastidy were sitting beside me on that tailgate today, she wouldn't want the final chapter of her story to be about one misunderstanding. She would probably tell me to quit carrying something I no longer had the power to change. She always had a way of seeing past the moment into what really mattered.

What really mattered wasn't our last conversation.

It was every conversation before it. It was every time she answered the phone. Every time she listened. Every time she stayed.

That's the story worth remembering.

When people die, we have a habit of reducing them to dates. Born. Died. A dash between two numbers. As if an entire human life could fit inside a line on a gravestone.

Chastidy deserves better than that.

She deserves to be remembered sitting on the tailgate of her brown Ford Ranger after working all day because someone she cared about shouldn't have to face the night alone.

She deserves to be remembered wrapped in a blanket beneath a sky that slowly surrendered to morning.

She deserves to be remembered for answering the phone years later when I couldn't understand my own grief.

She deserves to be remembered for telling me I deserved a good woman when I couldn't yet believe it myself.

She deserves to be remembered for her kindness. For her steadiness. For the quiet strength that never demanded attention.

This chapter is my attempt to make sure she is.

People often ask me why I write so much about trauma.

The answer has never been to explain suffering. It's to explain people.

Trauma may explain why we struggle. But people explain why we survive.

For all the books I've read... for all the research I've studied... for all the theories I've learned... I can still trace one of the most important lessons of my life back to a single night on a tailgate.

Healing doesn't always arrive as a breakthrough. Sometimes it arrives as another human being.

Sometimes the greatest act of love isn't finding the perfect words. Sometimes it's refusing to leave.

I've spent years trying to understand why that night mattered so much. I think I've finally figured it out.

That night didn't erase my trauma. It didn't cure my fear. It didn't undo my past.

What it did was challenge a belief my nervous system had carried for almost my entire life.

The belief that everyone leaves.

One night. One truck. One blanket. One woman.

That was all it took to introduce another possibility.

Maybe not everyone leaves. Maybe some people stay. Maybe love isn't measured by promises. Maybe it's measured by presence.

Maybe the people who change our lives the most aren't always the loudest. Sometimes they're the ones who quietly pull into the driveway at one o'clock in the morning because they know you shouldn't have to face the darkness by yourself.

I've often wondered whether Chastidy knew what she gave me that night.

I don't think she did.

I don't think she realized she would become part of the foundation for everything I would eventually learn about trauma, attachment, healing, and hope.

She probably thought she was just sitting beside a friend who needed someone.

But that's the remarkable thing about ordinary kindness. It rarely knows how far it will travel.

Years later, her compassion is still moving through these pages. It reaches every reader who has ever believed they were too broken to be loved. Every reader who has waited for a phone that never rang. Every reader who has mistaken silence for proof that they didn't matter.

If this book accomplishes anything, I hope it convinces at least one person that presence matters.

You don't always have to know what to say. You don't have to fix someone's life. You don't have to carry all their pain. Sometimes you just have to show up.

Sometimes surviving until morning begins with discovering you don't have to survive the night alone.

I started this chapter by telling you there are two kinds of people.

The ones who show up when life is easy. And the ones who show up when everything falls apart.

I've never forgotten which kind Chastidy was.

On the worst night of my life... when everyone else had gone silent... she came. She sat beside me. She stayed until sunrise.

Years later, she answered the phone again. She reminded me that grief has its own language. She reminded me that I deserved to be loved well. She reminded me, over and over again, that presence is sometimes the greatest gift one human being can give another.

I never got to say goodbye.

So let me say what I should have said while you were still here.

Thank you.

Thank you for answering the phone. Thank you for the brown Ford Ranger. Thank you for the tailgate. Thank you for the blanket. Thank you for staying when everyone else disappeared. Thank you for giving my nervous system one night of different evidence. Thank you for helping me survive long enough to become the man writing these words.

You once helped carry me through a single night. You never could have known that, years later, that same night would help carry thousands of readers who have lived through darkness of their own.

That is your legacy. Not the misunderstanding. Not the silence at the end. Not the day you died.

Your legacy is that, when another human being was drowning, you climbed into the water and stayed with him until morning.

Some people are medicine. Not because they fix everything.

Because they remind us that we were never meant to heal alone.

Her name was Chastidy Dodge.

She came.

And because she did...

I'm still here.

Chapter 20: Finding the Map in the Madness

A while earlier, before everything with Sarah that night, I'd made a stupid decision that nearly cost me everything. Someone had a box of junk at their placenot household trash, just random shit that looked useful. I was the type who'd haul stuff to the dump and come back with more than I left with. I said yeah, I'd take it. On the way I went through it and kept a few things instead of tossing everything.

Weeks later a buddy named Mike saw one of the bags and got quiet. "You better be careful with that," he said. "Those look like blood stains from a murder." I laughed it off at the time. Then months after that, another guy mentioned the FBI had been on some land looking for a body. That's when the panic hit. I remembered Mike's warning and burned the bag in a fire out back. Three days later I heard the knock.

I bolted out the side door and ended up at a house I knew. They let me use the bathroom. Five minutes later someone yelled that I was in there. Two cops: an investigator right in front of me and a deputy from another county positioned on my left flank. The setup felt off, deliberate. The investigator started drilling me. I answered everything straight. He accused me of lying, said he caught me in one, then claimed he'd review the recording. I told him to do itI wasn't lying. He came back and shifted: maybe I wasn't lying, but I was holding back, knew more than I was saying.

He kept pushing about "the black bag." Over and over. Whether on purpose or not, it triggered me. "The bag was fucking red," I snapped, "and I burned it." I told him someone had given me a box, gave him the broad strokes without names. He drove me to Sarah's place because I mentioned a couple things from the box that hadn't been thrown awaythey were in her truck. She wasn't home. She'd cleaned the truck out a few days earlier at the corner store, so we went to the dumpster. It was full. They started moving shit around but when it came time to climb in deeper, the investigator and deputy looked at each other and said they weren't getting in there. We left.

The deputy from the other county drove me home. He got out with a consent form and asked me to initial it so he could search my room. I told him it wasn't my house, he'd need to talk to my parents. He said no, just my room. He started reading the fine printif he saw anything illegal unrelated to what they were looking for, he could arrest me for it. I hesitated. He reassured me he wasn't there for drugs; if he saw any he'd put them back and act like he didn't see them. I didn't believe him for a second, but refusing meant more cops would show up. I initialed.

He tore my room apart looking for the bag I already told him I burned. Found needles, some bags, a couple balls in a drawer. He shut it like he promised and kept looking. Eventually he left. I had no idea if he was coming back, if I was about to get charged with burning evidence or whatever the fuck they could pin on me.

That started the longest six months of my life. I texted friends, freaking out, but nobody would talk to me. People heard there was a drug bust at my place on the main road and the silence was total. I never felt more alone. I did all the drugs, drank, partiedanything to drown the waiting. Every knock, every car slowing down, I thought this was it. Prison for something that started with a box of junk I should've never kept. Later I heard the guy had a heart attack in the woods by his house and they found the body. I never bought that story, but nothing else ever came of it.

The waiting itself was the sentence.

Chapter 20 – MY BODY KEPT THE SCORE (previously drafted as "The 30-Minute Trap")

You can tell yourself a story about pain if you need to. You can call it temporary. You can call it soreness. You can call it one more thing that will pass if you just keep moving.

But the body does not care what story the mind tells. The body keeps score anyway.

On June 7, 2025, what should have been another warrant arrest turned into something my dominant hand still has not fully recovered from.

It did not look dramatic from the outside. No movie scene. No beating. No blood running across the floor. Just officers, cuffs, my arm forced up behind my back, and that familiar feeling of being at the mercy of people who could leave damage behind and still call it routine.

I came out of the bathroom area without a fight. No tackle. No brawl. No cinematic resistance. On paper, it became another warrant service. Failure to appear. Failure to comply. Driving on suspended. Administrative language — the kind that can reduce an overwhelmed nervous system to a list of violations.

My body remembers it differently.

It remembers the cuffs tightening around my dominant right wrist. Too tight. It remembers the first sharp bolt of pain, and numbness arriving almost immediately after. It remembers my arm being forced upward behind my back until pain shot through my shoulder and turned my wrist into a live wire. It remembers the moment something stopped feeling right, even though the procedure just kept going.

That is the part people miss when they talk about force as though it only counts if it leaves a bruise big enough for a camera.

Sometimes the injury comes from compression. Sometimes from torque. Sometimes from a restraint held long enough that tissue starts paying a price the room won't acknowledge.

I was in cuffs long enough for my hand to stop feeling like it belonged to me. At first I couldn't separate pain from adrenaline — living with Complex PTSD complicates a moment like that. My nervous system tends to recognize danger before my mind has words for it, so in those first minutes I genuinely couldn't tell where panic ended and injury began.

But the symptoms didn't leave with the handcuffs.

Days later, my hand was still numb. Not the temporary kind that fades once circulation returns. Wrong numb. Deadened and electric at the same time. Tingling in every finger. My grip weakened. Signals that should have moved effortlessly from brain to fingers no longer arrived clean. Even light touch sometimes registered as pain.

I couldn't make a fist. I could close my hand about halfway before everything went tight. You could see it on the skin — the inside of my hand pulled into lines, like there were ropes stretched underneath that shouldn't have been visible.

Then came the humiliations that made the injury real in a different way.

Dropping tools. Losing my grip. I couldn't hold a screwdriver. I couldn't control a paintbrush. No longer trusting my own hand to do what it had always done without thought.

I've spent years doing property rehabilitation work — repairs, power tools, weed eaters, the kind of work where your hands aren't accessories. They're your livelihood, your rhythm, your proof that you can still build something useful out of wreckage. When my right hand stopped working right, it wasn't just pain. It was income. It was identity. It was one more system failure landing in a body already carrying too many.

By my own estimate, I lost roughly sixty-five percent of the function in my dominant hand. That's not a clinical number. That's a life number — what's left when you try to make a fist and can't, when you reach for a tool and hesitate, when the part of your body you depended on becomes something you now have to negotiate with. I felt like I had maybe thirty-five percent of my hand left. Not gone enough for strangers to see. Gone enough to alter everything.

There's a name for what can happen after tight cuffs held too long: handcuff neuropathy. Compression injury. Nerve entrapment from circumferential pressure around the wrist — and it isn't limited to the surface nerve most people think of. A 2000 prospective study of forty-one patients found neuropathies from overtightened handcuffs in twenty-two superficial radial nerves, twelve median nerves, and nine ulnar nerves.¹ Documented cases go back decades further than that.²

Learning that mattered. Not because it erased what happened, but because it told me the injury had a shape. It wasn't vague. It wasn't me being dramatic because I already had a trauma history. There are mechanisms for this. There are tests for it — nerve conduction studies, electromyography, functional evaluations.

I didn't get those tests right away.

No EMG. No nerve conduction study. No specialist. Just me, a hand that wouldn't come back online, and a life that kept asking me to function like nothing had changed.

People often assume delayed medical documentation means the injury wasn't serious. Trauma complicates that assumption.

I did see a primary care doctor. I got the referral. But the specialist was in Russellville, and I don't drive. The call had to be made, and I couldn't make it myself — my dad had to call for me. He called, and they scheduled the appointment.

Three months out from the day he made that call.

Three months of waiting while my hand stayed wrong. Three months of the calendar moving at its own slow pace while I lived with a grip that wouldn't come back. Three months of knowing something was wrong and being unable to do anything except wait for the system to catch up to what my body already knew.

Objective proof requires transportation, someone else to make the call when your voice won't work, and the ability to wait while the injury doesn't. The barrier wasn't motivation. The barrier was logistics, biology, and a system that doesn't move on trauma time.

The arrest lasted minutes.

My hand did not.

Over a year later — July 2026 now — I'm still trying to make a fist. Still trying to trust my own grip. Still trying to understand how something that looked routine from the outside could permanently change the way I move through the world.

I haven't worked since. Not because I stopped trying. The hand didn't heal clean, and somewhere in the same stretch of time I also tore a rotator cuff, and the agoraphobia and the Complex PTSD didn't take a break to let the rest of it heal. I can't start a push mower. I can't start a weedeater. The pull-cord needs a grip and a shoulder I don't have anymore, and those were the two things the work was built on.

That's not one injury. That's four things standing on top of each other, and any one of them alone would have been enough to take the job.

Paper can record an arrest. It can log the time, the charges, the officers involved. It can reduce a body to a booking number.

It cannot record what it feels like to still catch yourself flexing your right hand before you pick anything up, over a year later.

Thirty minutes in cuffs. A lifetime of compensating.

The system has a word for that kind of injury. The body has a different word for it. The body just calls it here — still here, still present, still reminding you every time you reach for something that the damage didn't leave when they let you go.

I can't tell you exactly how long I was in those cuffs.

But my hand remembers. It keeps the time.

Every single day.

The docket forced the confrontation. The writing turned it into testimony.

The nervous system learned early: danger erupts without warning, freezing is survival, connection is risky. Court dates, official mail, show-cause orders — all threat cues. The body does what it was trained to do. The systems do what they’re built to do.

August 10 is coming. I’ll carry the documentation — firing records, injury medicals, therapy notes, diagnosis timeline. But even if that hearing lands right, the wiring doesn’t vanish. The work continues. The writing continues. The ember continues.

This chapter isn’t the end. It’s another page in the map I started writing the day I finally understood why I couldn’t move that morning even though I knew I had to.

The docket is still open in places. So is the nervous system. So is the story.

Turn the page anyway.

A hand that still can't hold a screwdriver right. August 10, 2026, on a calendar I can't make disappear by not looking at it.

I don't know what happens that day. I could tell you I'll be fine. I won't tell you that, because I don't know it's true, and this book doesn't get to lie to you two pages before the end just to give you something to feel good about.

What I know is this: Maverick, Shayla, Teaguen, Adalee — I named them once already, back at the start of this book, and I'm naming them again here on purpose. Not as a device. Because everything in these docket chapters — the warrants, the freeze, the hand, the arrears — all of it sits on top of the fact that I am their father and I am not there the way a father should be. The system has its number for that. Six thousand nine hundred sixty dollars. That's not the number that matters to me. The number that matters is four.

I'm not writing this chapter to ask you to feel sorry for a man who missed court dates. I caused damage. Real damage, to real people, and the worst of it landed on the four people I'd protect with my last breath if my body would let me move when it counted. That's not something a diagnosis erases. C-PTSD explains the mechanism. It doesn't refund the cost.

But here's what's true on both sides of that: understanding why my body locked up in that doorway didn't happen so I could forgive myself for free. It happened so I could stop mistaking the freeze for who I am, and start telling the difference between what happened to me and what I do next.

Next is August 10. Next is whatever comes after August 10, win or lose. Next is showing up for my kids in the only way I actually can right now — on paper, in these pages, telling the truth instead of another version of events built to make me look better than I was.

I don't get a clean ending. The docket doesn't offer one. But I get to keep writing the next page, and the one after that, whether the door swings the right way on August 10 or it doesn't.

That's not alignment. That's not healed. That's just still here, still theirs, still writing it straight.

Turn the page.
